
# 🎼 Quick Composition Guide (Concert Pitch, C Major)

A fast, practical reference for writing simple, coherent music using **melody + harmony + rhythm**, designed for easy import into notation software and later transposition.

---

## 1. Why Work in C Major (Concert Pitch)

* No sharps or flats → faster decisions
* Reduces theory overhead → focus on structure
* Easy to transpose later

**Workflow:**

1. Write everything in C major
2. Make it sound good
3. Transpose for instruments later

---

## 2. Core Scale & Chords

### C Major Scale

C – D – E – F – G – A – B

### Diatonic Chords

| Degree | Chord | Function               |
| ------ | ----- | ---------------------- |
| I      | C     | Home / resolution      |
| ii     | Dm    | Pre-dominant           |
| iii    | Em    | Passing                |
| IV     | F     | Expansion / lift       |
| V      | G     | Tension                |
| vi     | Am    | Relative minor / color |
| vii°   | Bdim  | Leading tone (rare)    |

---

## 3. Plug-and-Play Chord Progressions

### Default Progression

```
C – G – Am – F
(I – V – vi – IV)
```

### Basic Phrase

```
C – F – G – C
(I – IV – V – I)
```

### Strong Ending

```
Dm – G – C
(ii – V – I)
```

### Slightly Richer

```
Am – Dm – G – C
(vi – ii – V – I)
```

---

## 4. Melody Writing Rules (High Impact)

### Rule 1: Target Chord Tones on Strong Beats

Strong beats = 1 and 3 (in 4/4)

Chord tones = 1st, 3rd, 5th of chord

Example (C chord):

* Good: C, E, G on strong beats
* Passing tones allowed between

---

### Rule 2: Use Stepwise Motion

* Mostly move by step (C → D → E)
* Use leaps sparingly for emphasis

---

### Rule 3: Shape Phrases

* Start low → rise → resolve
* Think in **4-bar phrases**

---

### Rule 4: Repetition + Variation

Repeat motifs but change:

* Ending note
* Rhythm
* Direction

---

## 5. Supporting Voice (Alto / Left Hand / Harmony Layer)

### Option A: Sustained Harmony (Easiest)

* Whole or half notes
* Play root or 3rd of chord

Example:

* C chord → play C or E
* F chord → play F or A

---

### Option B: Counter-Melody (More Musical)

Rules:

* Move **opposite direction** of melody
* Avoid same rhythm as melody
* Use longer note values

---

### Option C: Broken Chords (Arpeggios)

Instead of holding:

* C → play C–E–G pattern
* Adds motion without clutter

---

## 6. Rhythm Basics (Critical)

### Keep It Simple First

* Use mostly:

  * Quarter notes
  * Eighth notes

---

### Strong vs Weak Beats

| Beat | Strength |
| ---- | -------- |
| 1    | Strong   |
| 2    | Weak     |
| 3    | Medium   |
| 4    | Weak     |

👉 Important notes go on strong beats

---

### Avoid Common Problems

❌ Too many accents
❌ Random syncopation
❌ All notes same length

---

### Use Rhythm for Interest

Instead of changing notes, try:

* Repeat note, change rhythm
* Add rests
* Syncopation (occasionally)

---

## 7. 4-Bar Writing Template (Use This Every Time)

### Step 1: Pick progression

```
Bar 1: C
Bar 2: F
Bar 3: G
Bar 4: C
```

---

### Step 2: Write melody

* Use chord tones on beats 1 & 3
* Fill with passing notes

---

### Step 3: Add harmony

* Whole notes (easy mode)
  or
* Simple counter-line

---

### Step 4: Fix rhythm

* Add variation
* Remove clutter

---

## 8. Fast Checklist

Before you finish:

* ✅ Are strong beats chord tones?
* ✅ Is there a clear phrase (start → peak → end)?
* ✅ Does harmony match melody?
* ✅ Is rhythm consistent but not boring?
* ✅ Does it resolve (not just stop)?

---

## 9. Transposing Later (Sax Example)

After writing in C:

* Bb instrument → up major 2nd
* Eb instrument → up major 6th

Most notation software does this automatically.

---

## 10. Key Principle

> **Clarity beats complexity.**

A simple, well-structured idea will always sound better than a complex but unfocused one.

# 🎼 Continuation: Practical Examples & Sax Duet Application (C Major)

---

# 11. Bar-by-Bar Example (Good vs Bad Writing)

We’ll use this progression:

```
C – F – G – C
```

---

## ❌ Weak Version (Common Beginner Issues)

### Bar 1 (C)

* Melody: random notes (D–F–B–A)
* Problem:

  * No clear chord tones
  * No tonal center

### Bar 2 (F)

* Melody jumps erratically (C → A → F → D)
* Problem:

  * Large, unmotivated leaps
  * No phrase direction

### Bar 3 (G)

* Same rhythm repeated (all eighth notes)
* Problem:

  * Rhythm is monotonous
  * No emphasis on strong beats

### Bar 4 (C)

* Ends on D
* Problem:

  * No resolution → sounds unfinished

---

## ✅ Improved Version (Structured Approach)

### Bar 1 (C)

* Melody: **E – D – C – G**
* Why it works:

  * Starts on chord tone (E)
  * Stepwise motion
  * Ends stable

---

### Bar 2 (F)

* Melody: **F – A – G – F**
* Why it works:

  * Strong beat = F (root)
  * A adds color (3rd)
  * Smooth contour

---

### Bar 3 (G)

* Melody: **G – B – A – G**
* Why it works:

  * B (leading tone) adds tension
  * Clear dominant function

---

### Bar 4 (C)

* Melody: **E – D – C (held)**
* Why it works:

  * Resolves to tonic
  * Longer final note = closure

---

## Key Takeaways

* Strong beats = chord tones
* Stepwise motion > random leaps
* Rhythm should **support phring**, not fight it
* Ending must **resolve to tonic**

---

# 12. Rhythm Fix Example

## ❌ Before

```
C C C C | D D D D | E E E E | C C C C
(all same rhythm)
```

## ✅ After

```
C (quarter) D E (eighths) | G (half) rest |
F A (eighths) G F | E (whole)
```

### Improvements:

* Mixed note lengths
* Strategic rests
* Natural phrasing

# 13. Sax Duet Application (Soprano + Alto)

We now apply everything to a **2-part texture**.

---

## Role Assignment

### Soprano Sax

* Primary melody
* More movement
* Higher register

### Alto Sax

* Support role:

  * Harmony OR
  * Counter-melody

---

## Option 1: Simple Harmony (Best Starting Point)

### Chords:

```
C – F – G – C
```

### Soprano:

* Plays melody (as above)

### Alto:

* Whole notes:

| Bar | Note |
| --- | ---- |
| 1   | C    |
| 2   | F    |
| 3   | G    |
| 4   | C    |

---

### Why this works:

* Clear harmonic foundation
* No rhythmic conflict
* Clean texture

---

## Option 2: Basic Counter-Melody

### Rules applied:

* Slower rhythm than soprano
* Opposite motion
* Avoid unison movement

---

### Example

#### Bar 1 (C)

* Soprano: E–D–C–G
* Alto: G (half) → E (half)

👉 Contrary motion

---

#### Bar 2 (F)

* Soprano: F–A–G–F
* Alto: C (whole)

👉 Stable anchor

---

#### Bar 3 (G)

* Soprano: G–B–A–G
* Alto: D → G

👉 Reinforces dominant

---

#### Bar 4 (C)

* Soprano: E–D–C
* Alto: C (whole)

👉 Strong resolution

---

## Option 3: Broken-Chord Support (More Motion)

Instead of long notes, alto plays:

### Bar 1 (C)

C–E–G–E

### Bar 2 (F)

F–A–C–A

### Bar 3 (G)

G–B–D–B

### Bar 4 (C)

C–G–E–C

---

### When to use this:

* Texture feels too empty
* You want gentle movement

---

# 14. Common Duet Problems

### ❌ Both parts too busy

→ Sounds cluttered

### ❌ Both parts same rhythm

→ Sounds robotic

### ❌ No harmonic agreement

→ Sounds wrong

---

## Fix Strategy

* One part leads, one supports
* Contrast rhythm
* Align on chord tones

---

# 15. Minimal Working Template (Sax Duet)

Use this every time:

### Step 1: Chords

```
C – F – G – C
```

### Step 2: Soprano

* Write melody using:

  * chord tones on strong beats
  * stepwise motion

### Step 3: Alto

Choose ONE:

* Whole notes (safe)
* Counter-line (moderate)
* Arpeggios (active)

---

# 16. Final Checklist (Duet Version)

* ✅ Melody clearly leads
* ✅ Harmony supports (not competes)
* ✅ Rhythms are different but compatible
* ✅ Strong beats align with chords
* ✅ Ending resolves clearly

---

# Final Principle

> **Good music = clear melody + supportive harmony + controlled rhythm**

If one is weak, the whole thing feels weak.
